# Landing Page Copy

Write every section of a landing page in conversion order, driven by one offer and one action. Copy first, layout second — the page structure below maps to any template or builder.

## Workflow

1. **Lock the fundamentals.** Identify: the offer, the visitor (who arrives and from where), the single conversion action (sign up, book a demo, buy), and the top objection. If the user hasn't said what makes the product different, ask for or infer the sharpest differentiator — the page fails without it.
2. **Write the hero.**
   - Headline: the outcome the visitor gets, in their words, under 10 words. Not the product category, not a pun.
   - Subheadline: how it works or who it's for, 1–2 sentences.
   - Primary CTA button: verb + outcome ("Start free trial", not "Submit").
   - Optional: one line of risk-reversal under the button ("No credit card required").
3. **Build the body in this order:**
   1. Social proof strip (logos, count, or a one-line stat)
   2. Problem section — name the pain in the visitor's language
   3. Solution/features — 3–4 blocks, each: benefit heading, 2-sentence explanation, feature named last
   4. How it works — 3 numbered steps
   5. Testimonial(s) — with name, role, and a specific result; write realistic placeholders if none provided and mark them clearly as PLACEHOLDER
   6. Objection-handling FAQ — 4–6 questions, including pricing and "what if it doesn't work for me"
   7. Final CTA section — restate the outcome + button
4. **Do a friction pass.** Reread and cut every sentence that doesn't move the visitor toward the action. Replace jargon with the words a customer would use in a review.

## Output format

Markdown with a heading per page section in order, button labels in **bold**, and `[PLACEHOLDER: ...]` markers for anything requiring real customer data.

## Quality bar

- One page, one CTA. If the user wants two actions, make one primary and one text link.
- Every benefit claim must be concrete enough to be falsifiable ("cut reporting time from 3 hours to 10 minutes" beats "save time").
- Headlines are judged by a stranger test: would someone who never heard of the company understand the offer in 5 seconds?
