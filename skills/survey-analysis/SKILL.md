# Survey Analysis

Two jobs, one discipline: design surveys whose answers are usable, and analyze results without overclaiming. Most survey damage happens at design time — a biased question can't be fixed in analysis.

## Design workflow

1. **Start from decisions, not questions.** List what the user will *decide* based on results; every question must feed a decision or get cut. Target under 10 questions / 5 minutes — completion drops and answer quality degrades beyond that.
2. **Write unbiased questions.** One concept per question (split double-barrels: "satisfied with price and quality" is two questions), neutral phrasing (no "how much do you love…"), balanced scales (equal positive/negative options, labeled points, consistent direction), and "not applicable / prefer not to say" where honesty requires an exit. Standard instruments where they exist (NPS wording, Likert 5-point) beat inventions — they come with benchmarks.
3. **Order and pilot.** Easy/engaging first, sensitive/demographic last, one open-ended "anything else?" at the end. Recommend piloting on 3–5 people to catch ambiguity before launch. Note sampling: who's invited, and what non-response will do to interpretation.

## Analysis workflow

1. **Report the response reality first.** n, response rate, and completion rate — and what the non-respondents likely have in common (the unhappy and the busy skip surveys differently). Results from a 12% response rate get framed as directional, not definitive.
2. **Analyze by question type.** Frequencies with n for categoricals; means *and* distributions for scales (a polarized 1-and-5 pattern averages to a misleading 3); NPS as % promoters − % detractors with segment cuts. For small samples, report counts alongside percentages — "3 of 8" not "37.5%".
3. **Cross-tab where decisions live.** Break key results by the segments the user acts on (department, plan, tenure). Flag differences as notable only when segment sizes support it; call out n<30 cells explicitly rather than narrating noise.
4. **Code the open-ends.** Theme the verbatims (bottom-up), count theme frequency, and pull 2–3 representative quotes per major theme — anonymized. Open-ends often contain the "why" behind every closed-question surprise.
5. **Deliver decisions, not just charts.** Findings ranked by decision-relevance, each with: the numbers, the caveat, and the recommended action. Include what the survey *cannot* answer and what follow-up (interviews, a/b test) would close the gap.

## Output format

For design: the question list with scale specs, flow order, and pilot/sampling notes. For analysis: response-quality header → key findings (ranked, with numbers and caveats) → segment cuts → open-end themes with quotes → recommendations → limitations.

## Quality bar

- Never imply causation from cross-sectional correlation; say "associated with," and flag confounders.
- Every chart or table states its n.
- Anonymity promises made at collection are honored in reporting — no quotes traceable to individuals in small segments.
