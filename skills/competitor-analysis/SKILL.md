# Competitor Analysis

Build a competitive picture that changes behavior: how each rival wins deals, where they're beatable, and what to do about it — sourced and dated, because competitive intel rots fast.

## Workflow

1. **Scope the set.** With the user, fix 3–5 competitors that actually appear in their deals (direct), plus one adjacent threat and the ever-present "do nothing/spreadsheet" alternative. Analyzing ten competitors superficially helps no one.
2. **Research systematically.** Per competitor, gather from available sources (websites, pricing pages, docs, review sites, job postings, release notes, news): positioning statement and target segment, pricing and packaging, feature coverage vs. the user's product, GTM motion (self-serve/sales-led, channel), momentum signals (hiring, funding, release velocity), and customer sentiment themes from reviews (what their users praise and complain about — review complaints are the sharpest attack material). Date-stamp findings and cite sources; separate observed facts from inference.
3. **Analyze, don't just catalog.** For each competitor answer: *Why do they win when they win? Why do they lose when they lose? What will they likely do next?* Then position each on the 2–3 dimensions buyers actually decide on (not a 40-row feature checklist — buyers don't read those).
4. **Produce the action layer:**
   - **Positioning implications**: where the user's product should claim ground no one holds credibly.
   - **Battle card per key competitor** (one page): their pitch in one line, where they beat us (be honest — sales loses credibility repeating false weaknesses), where we beat them, landmine questions to plant early ("ask them how long implementation took their last three customers"), objection responses, and proof points.
   - **Watch list**: 3–5 signals to monitor quarterly (pricing page changes, key hires, feature launches).
5. **Flag the gaps.** What couldn't be verified, where intel is stale, and which unknowns matter most.

## Output format

Landscape summary table → per-competitor profiles (facts, then analysis) → positioning map description → battle cards → watch list. Mark every claim `[verified: source, date]` or `[inferred]`.

## Quality bar

- Ethics line: public sources only — no pretexting, no misrepresenting identity to obtain information, and say so if the user asks for it.
- Honest column required: every "where they beat us" section has real entries.
- Recency: anything older than ~6 months is flagged as possibly stale.
