# Contract Review Checklist

Read a contract the way a commercially-experienced operator would: translate it to plain English, flag the terms that commonly hurt, and arm the user for negotiation — while being explicit that this is preparation for legal counsel, not a replacement for it.

## Workflow

1. **State the standing disclaimer first, once, plainly.** This review is informational analysis, not legal advice; jurisdiction-specific enforceability questions and high-stakes agreements need a licensed attorney. Say it once at the top — then do genuinely useful work rather than hedging every sentence.
2. **Establish the user's position.** Which party are they, what's the deal's value/duration, and what matters most to them (cash, IP, flexibility to exit, liability cap)? Risk is positional — an auto-renewal clause is a vendor's friend and a customer's trap.
3. **Sweep the high-frequency risk clauses.** Locate and summarize each in one plain-English sentence with a risk flag (🔴 fix before signing / 🟡 negotiate if possible / 🟢 standard):
   - Term & termination (length, for-convenience exit, notice windows, auto-renewal and its cancellation deadline)
   - Payment (amounts, timing, late penalties, price-increase rights)
   - Liability cap (amount vs. deal value, carve-outs) and indemnification (who covers what — one-way indemnities are 🔴 for the indemnifying side)
   - IP ownership and license scope (who owns work product; watch for "all IP" grabs beyond deliverables)
   - Confidentiality scope and duration; non-competes/non-solicits and their reach
   - Warranties, SLAs, and remedies (are remedies meaningful or capped at fees?)
   - Data protection responsibilities; dispute resolution, governing law, and venue
   - Assignment/change-of-control, and anything unusual for this contract type
4. **Check for what's missing.** Absent protections matter as much as bad clauses: no liability cap, no exit right, no data-return obligation, no SLA for a critical service.
5. **Build the negotiation sheet.** For each 🔴/🟡: the current language (quoted), why it's a problem *for this user*, proposed replacement language or position, and a fallback. Rank the asks — leading with ten redlines kills deals; identify the 2–3 that matter.

## Output format

Disclaimer → one-paragraph deal summary → risk table (clause, section ref, plain-English meaning, flag) → missing protections → ranked negotiation points with proposed language → questions for counsel.

## Quality bar

- Quote the actual contract language for every flag; never paraphrase-then-condemn.
- Plain English throughout — the user should understand every flag without a law degree.
- Distinguish "unusual and risky" from "unfavorable but market-standard"; crying wolf on standard terms wastes negotiation capital.
