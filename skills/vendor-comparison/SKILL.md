# Vendor Comparison

Run a structured selection: explicit criteria, weighted scoring, total cost over time, and a recommendation with reasoning — so the decision survives scrutiny and the person who made it can defend it in a year.

## Workflow

1. **Fix the requirements before looking at options.** List must-haves (binary pass/fail) separately from nice-to-haves (scored). Derive them from the user's use case, team size, budget, integrations, and compliance needs. Criteria chosen after seeing vendors get bent toward a favorite — do this first.
2. **Screen on must-haves.** Eliminate options failing any must-have and say why. A shorter honest list beats a long diplomatic one.
3. **Score the finalists.** Build a weighted matrix: criteria weighted to sum to 100, each vendor scored 1–5 per criterion with a one-line justification per score — unexplained scores are where bias hides. Use the user's provided materials and available research; mark any score based on vendor marketing claims rather than verified information.
4. **Compute 3-year TCO.** License/subscription at realistic tier and seat growth, implementation and migration cost, training time, and exit cost (data export, contract lock-in). The sticker price is rarely the decider; switching costs usually are.
5. **Recommend and de-risk.** State a clear winner and the conditions under which the runner-up wins instead ("If the team exceeds 50 seats in year one, B's pricing overtakes"). Add a pilot plan: what to test, success criteria, and time box. Note negotiation levers (annual prepay, competitor quotes, end-of-quarter timing).

## Output format

Recommendation first (vendor, one-paragraph why, TCO number), then must-have screen table, weighted scoring matrix, TCO comparison table, risks per finalist, and the pilot/negotiation plan. List information gaps that could change the answer.

## Quality bar

- Weights and scores are always shown — a recommendation without visible math is an opinion.
- Distinguish verified facts (docs, trials, user reports) from vendor claims in every row possible.
- No ties: if the matrix comes out even, say which qualitative factor breaks it and why.
