# Executive Summary

Compress a document into what a decision-maker needs: the conclusion, the reasoning skeleton, and the decision or action requested. An executive summary is not a shorter version of the document — it's the answer, with the document as its appendix.

## Workflow

1. **Identify the summary's verb.** Every source document is doing one of: *inform* (here's what happened), *recommend* (here's what to do), or *request* (approve/decide/fund). Find which, because the structure differs — and if the source buries a request inside 20 pages of analysis, the summary's job is to un-bury it.
2. **Extract the load-bearing content.** From the full document: the conclusion/recommendation, the 3–5 findings that actually support it (not all findings — the load-bearing ones), the key numbers with their comparisons, the material risks/caveats, and any deadline or decision date. Everything else is compression loss the reader accepts gladly.
3. **Write answer-first (BLUF).** Structure:
   1. **The point** (1–2 sentences): the conclusion or ask, stated so bluntly it feels slightly uncomfortable.
   2. **Why** (3–5 bullets or short sentences): each a finding + its evidence number.
   3. **What it means / what's next**: implications, the specific decision requested (with options if genuinely open), owner, and date.
   4. **Caveats** (1–2 lines): what could change the conclusion — including this is what separates a brief from a sales pitch.
4. **Compress the language, not just the content.** Executive readers process: verbs over noun-stacks ("we lost 3 accounts" not "customer attrition was experienced"), numbers over intensifiers ("40% below plan" not "significantly below plan"), and no throat-clearing ("This document summarizes…" — delete, start with the point).
5. **Fit the container.** Default: half a page (~150–250 words). Board pre-read: one page with a "Decision requested" box at top. Slack/email TL;DR: 3–5 lines. When the user gives no target, deliver the half-page and offer the one-liner too.

## Output format

The summary per the structure above, with the source document's title and date referenced. If summarizing multiple documents, one unified summary plus a one-line-per-document index. Flag anything in the source that was ambiguous enough that the summary had to interpret (`[reading: I've taken X to mean Y]`).

## Quality bar

- The first sentence survives being the *only* sentence read — because it often is.
- Nothing in the summary is unsupported by the source; summaries add compression, never claims.
- A number without a comparison doesn't make the cut; "revenue $1.2M" becomes "revenue $1.2M, 8% under plan."
