# Discovery Call Prep

Produce a one-page prep sheet the user can glance at during the call: who they're meeting, what to ask, what to listen for, and how to close for the next step.

## Workflow

1. **Research the account.** Use whatever the user provides (company name, attendee names, notes, emails) and any available web access to assemble: what the company does, size/stage, recent news or triggers (funding, hires, launches), and the attendee's role and likely priorities. Mark anything inferred as (inferred).
2. **Form 2–3 pain hypotheses.** Based on the persona and company stage, state the problems they most plausibly have that the user's product solves. These drive the questioning — discovery is hypothesis testing, not an interrogation checklist.
3. **Build the question track.** 8–10 open questions in a deliberate arc:
   - Situation (1–2 max — don't waste call time on Googleable facts)
   - Problem ("walk me through how you handle X today", "what's hardest about that?")
   - Impact ("what does that cost you in time/revenue?", "who else feels this?")
   - Decision ("how have you bought tools like this before?", "who signs off?")
   - Timeline ("what happens if this isn't fixed by Q4?")
4. **Prep the hard moments.** List the 3 most likely objections (price, timing, incumbent/status quo) with a 2-sentence response each: acknowledge → reframe with a proof point. Add the answer to "so what do you guys cost?" so pricing talk doesn't wobble.
5. **Script the close.** The exact sentence proposing the next step with a date ("If this feels useful, the next step is a 45-minute demo with your ops lead — does Thursday work?").

## Output format

```
# Call prep: [Company] — [date]
Attendees: ... | Their likely goal: ...
Company snapshot: 3 bullets
Pain hypotheses: 1) ... 2) ... 3) ...
Questions: (grouped by arc)
Objections & answers: ...
Next-step close: "..."
Don't forget: [1–2 user-specific reminders]
```

## Quality bar

- Everything fits on one page — it's a glance sheet, not a dossier.
- Questions must be open and non-leading; cut any question answerable with "yes."
- Never fabricate company facts; separate researched facts from hypotheses explicitly.
