# Business Case

Build the document that gets a yes from someone accountable for the money: quantified benefits with visible assumptions, honest costs including the hidden ones, alternatives fairly compared, and a specific ask.

## Workflow

1. **Frame the problem in money first.** What does the status quo cost per year (hours × loaded rate, revenue leaked, risk exposure)? A business case that starts with the solution invites debate about the solution; one that starts with a quantified problem invites debate about which fix — a much better fight.
2. **Compare real alternatives.** Minimum three: do nothing (with its quantified ongoing cost — never let status quo look free), the recommended option, and one credible alternative (cheaper tool, partial fix, hire vs. outsource). Strawman alternatives are transparent and cost credibility.
3. **Quantify benefits conservatively, in tiers:**
   - **Hard savings** (budget line goes down): count fully.
   - **Productivity gains** (time freed): count at 50% unless the freed time maps to a named use — executives discount "soft hours" reflexively, so pre-discount them yourself and say so.
   - **Revenue upside**: probability-weight it, and show the case still clears the bar *without* it when possible.
   Every number's assumption is visible and adjustable ("32 staff × 3 hrs/week × $54 loaded rate × 46 weeks").
4. **Cost everything.** Purchase/subscription, implementation, migration, training and the productivity dip during it, ongoing admin, and exit costs. A 20–30% contingency on implementation for anything non-trivial. Three-year view, not year one.
5. **Compute the decision metrics.** Payback period (the number executives remember), 3-year net benefit, and ROI %. Add a sensitivity line: the result if benefits come in at 60% — a case that survives its own downside is the one that gets funded.
6. **Close with risks and the ask.** Top 3 risks with mitigations (including "implementation fails" — the most common one), what was deliberately left out of the benefits math (credibility deposit), and the specific ask: amount, timing, owner, first milestone, and the review point where the investment gets re-evaluated against promised benefits.

## Output format

One-page executive summary (problem cost, recommendation, payback, 3-yr net, the ask) → alternatives comparison table → benefits build-up with assumptions → full cost table → sensitivity → risks → implementation outline with the benefits-review checkpoint. Assumptions in one editable block.

## Quality bar

- Every benefit traces to an assumption the reader can challenge and change; no black-box ROI numbers.
- "Do nothing" has a cost line, always.
- The summary page stands alone — approvers who read one page can decide.
