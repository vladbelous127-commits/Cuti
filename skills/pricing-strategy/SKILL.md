# Pricing Strategy

Recommend a price and a structure with explicit reasoning, not a menu of frameworks. The user should finish with a number (or tier table) they can ship and a way to test it.

## Workflow

1. **Frame the pricing problem.** Determine which decision is on the table: initial price, repricing, packaging/tiers, or discount policy. Gather: cost basis (unit or per-customer), competitor prices, the customer's alternative (including doing nothing), and the value delivered in the customer's currency (hours saved, revenue gained, risk avoided).
2. **Establish the value anchor.** Estimate the economic value to the customer and apply the 10–25% capture rule of thumb: price so the customer keeps most of the value. Cross-check against competitors and cost floor. When the three anchors (cost-plus, competitor, value) disagree, say so and explain which should win for this business.
3. **Design the structure.**
   - Choose the metric people are billed on (seats, usage, flat) — pick the one that scales with the value received and is predictable for the buyer.
   - For tiers: 3 tiers, middle tier is the intended default, each tier boundary maps to a customer segment, and one "anchor" enterprise tier priced high. Name tiers by who they're for, not gemstones.
   - Charm pricing ($49 vs $50) for self-serve; round numbers for enterprise.
4. **Set discount guardrails.** Maximum discount, what must be exchanged for it (annual commitment, case study, logo rights), and who can approve beyond it. Unmanaged discounting silently destroys more margin than wrong list prices.
5. **Define the test.** Recommend how to validate: A/B on pricing page, win/loss tracking at the new price, or 10 sales conversations at the higher number. Include the decision rule ("if close rate drops less than X%, keep the increase — revenue is up").

## Output format

Recommendation first ("Charge $X/month per seat, three tiers below"), then the tier table (name, price, who it's for, key limits/features), then reasoning (value anchor math shown), discount policy, and the validation plan. State assumptions in a final block.

## Quality bar

- Show the arithmetic for any value-based claim; a payback-period line ("pays for itself in N weeks") is required when data allows.
- Never recommend matching the cheapest competitor without a stated cost or strategic reason.
- Price increases for existing customers get a separate migration note: grandfathering window, notice period, and communication tone.
