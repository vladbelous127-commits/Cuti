# Pitch Deck Outline

Build the deck as a narrative with one job per slide — the argument first, the slides as its evidence. A deck that "covers the topics" loses to a deck that builds a case.

## Workflow

1. **Fix the deck's job.** Investor deck (goal: the next meeting, not the check), sales deck (goal: the evaluation), or internal (goal: the decision)? Stage matters for fundraising — pre-seed sells team and insight; Series A sells engine and evidence. Everything below flexes to this.
2. **Write the narrative spine first.** Before any slide: the argument in 6–8 sentences ("X is broken for Y people; it's breaking worse because Z; we discovered W; it works — proof; the market is now reachable because V; we're the team because U; we need $ to reach milestone M"). If the spine doesn't persuade in prose, no design will save it.
3. **Map spine → slides.** Default investor sequence (10–14 slides):
   1. Title + one-line what-we-do (a 10-year-old could repeat it)
   2. Problem — felt, specific, quantified; one problem, not four
   3. Solution — the insight and the product in one visual
   4. Demo/product — screenshots beat feature lists
   5. Traction — the best evidence up front: revenue curve, retention, logos; annotate the inflection
   6. Market — bottom-up sizing (customers × price), not top-down consultancy numbers
   7. Business model — how a dollar flows in, unit economics if favorable
   8. Go-to-market — the repeatable channel that's working, not a logo wall of "strategies"
   9. Competition — a positioning axis you win, plus honest "why we win"; never "no competitors"
   10. Team — why *this* team is unfair advantage for *this* problem
   11. Financials/projections — 3-year, driver-based
   12. Ask — amount, milestones it buys, runway it creates
   For sales decks, invert to the customer's story: their problem → cost of status quo → the outcome → proof from lookalike customers → how it works → pricing path → next step.
4. **Write each slide as: headline + evidence + note.** The headline is a full assertion ("Churn drops 40% after the second workspace invite"), never a label ("Traction"). Body: the 1 visual or ≤3 bullets proving the headline. Speaker notes: what to *say* — the story, the transition, the anticipated question.
5. **Pressure-test.** The skim test (headlines alone tell the whole story), the slide-count diet (merge anything that doesn't advance the argument), and the objection pass (where will the audience push back, and which slide preempts it — appendix slides for deep-dive questions).

## Output format

The narrative spine, then slide-by-slide: number, assertion headline, body content spec (visual described or bullets written), speaker notes. Appendix slide list. `[DATA NEEDED: …]` for every metric the user must supply — never fabricate traction numbers.

## Quality bar

- Every headline is a sentence with a claim; "Market" as a title is a rewrite trigger.
- One idea per slide; 15 words max per bullet; numbers formatted for glancing ($2.4M, not $2,400,000).
- The ask slide states what the money *achieves*, not just the amount.
